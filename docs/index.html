Managing your AI like a team, not a tool

Donna OS turns Claude Code from one assistant doing everything into a small organization: a manager who routes work, specialists who go deep, and a verifier that catches the confident-but-wrong answer before you ever see it. It ran, across two months, as the validation layer for a real production product.

The problem I actually had

We were a small team shipping a real product, EdLM, an AI tutor for Indian K-12 students, heading into a live beta with a Chennai cohort. And a lot of our day-to-day was repetitive: review this diff, check whether a task was really finished, sanity-check that a change didn't quietly break something else. It was exactly the kind of work an AI can do, and I didn't want to pile it on the people. I wanted to hand it to the AI, so the humans stayed on the hard parts.

But when I reached for Claude Code's own multi-agent tools, I hit a wall. Ask a single assistant to spin up sub-agents or run a loop, and each sub-agent starts cold. It doesn't carry the full context of what we're building, what the task was, or what just changed. You get confident answers assembled from partial pictures.

And nothing checked the work. Ask "is this auth change safe to ship?" and you get one answer, in one voice, with no second opinion. When it was right, great. When it was subtly wrong, it sounded exactly the same: it said it reviewed the file, it said it tested the flow. No one's job was to disagree, and no one's job was to check whether the work claimed had actually been done.

Real teams don't work like one brilliant generalist. They have a manager who knows who to ask, specialists who go deep in their lane, and (the part everyone forgets) someone who double-checks the work before it ships. I wanted that shape, for our AI.

The idea

Stop treating the model as a solo developer. Treat it as an organization.

You talk to one agent: Donna. Donna is the manager, the only agent allowed to dispatch others. She reads your question, decides which specialists it actually needs, and sends them off, in parallel when she can. Each specialist is its own agent with its own role, its own tools, and its own memory: a code reviewer, a QA lead, a security officer, an architect, a researcher, a debugger, and a dozen more. When they report back, a verifier checks their claims for fabrication. Then Donna folds everything into one answer.

Two things make her more than a dispatcher. First, Donna holds the context. Because she only ever reads, never writes, she can range freely across the whole codebase, the team hub, and the task tracker to assemble the full picture, then hand each specialist exactly the slice it needs. The sub-agents stop starting cold; the manager briefs them. That one move is what fixed the context problem that sent me down this path in the first place.

Second, it costs nothing at the margin. The whole thing runs inside Claude Code, on each person's own subscription, with no API keys and no metered calls. Every teammate runs Donna on their own machine with their own CLI. Adding a manager and seventeen specialists to the team added zero to the monthly bill.

The loop

A single question doesn't just fan out once. It runs through rounds until the answer converges:

The engine runs each specialist as its own operating-system process (claude -p), so a round genuinely runs in parallel rather than one after another. Between rounds, an evaluate step asks the questions a good manager asks: do any two specialists contradict each other? did something surface a deeper question? was a finding too vague to act on? If so, it re-dispatches, carrying the previous round's findings forward. A hard cap (three rounds) means it converges instead of spinning.

Look at what happened, because every part of the thesis is in this one run:

  • Consensus made the real bugs trustworthy. Three specialists, working independently, converged on the same two critical issues with the same file-and-line evidence. When three separate agents point at the same line, you stop wondering if it's a hallucination. Both were found and fixed before launch.
  • Diversity caught what consensus couldn't. The security officer found a third issue, an account-resurrection vector, that neither of the other two surfaced. One generalist assistant would never have produced that angle. A specialist looking only for security did.
  • The verifier caught the specialists themselves. This is the part that matters most. The verifier didn't just trust the reports. It re-read the tool-call traces and caught three real inaccuracies in the specialists' own claims. Not product bugs, but bugs in what the agents said they did. That's the difference between a system that sounds authoritative and one you can actually trust.

The verifier is the whole point

Multi-agent systems have a dangerous property: they amplify hallucination. One agent's confident guess becomes another agent's input, and the error compounds with each hop. If you're going to run a team of AIs, the single most important thing you can build is the thing that pushes back.

So the verifier is a dedicated agent whose only job is to catch fabrication before it reaches you. It doesn't take a specialist's word for anything. It re-runs an independent check, and it reads the trace: if a specialist says "I reviewed 8 files" but the trace shows 3 reads, that's flagged. If it says "tested on mobile" but only desktop calls appear, that's flagged too. Every claim comes back tagged VERIFIED, UNVERIFIED, or FABRICATED.

Over time the verifier built up a taxonomy of the ways an AI specialist bluffs, so the manager can tighten the prompts that produce each one:

Fabrication typeWhat it looks likeCounter-measure
Over-coverageClaims to have reviewed N files, actually read fewerForce "list every file you read" in the output
Phantom-toolClaims a tool call that never appears in the traceRequire trace inspection before claiming success
Viewport-inventionClaims mobile or tablet testing with no viewport callRequire a screenshot per viewport
Cited-wrong-lineCites file:line, but the line doesn't say thatRe-read the cited line; mismatch means fabricated
Vague-evidence"There appears to be a bug" with no locationMark UNVERIFIED, ask for specifics
Phantom-findingReports a bug that doesn't reproduceRequire reproduction steps

Read-only by design

Here is a deliberate decision that people expect to be a limitation and is actually the point: Donna OS observes, audits, and reports. It does not change your code. The specialists read the codebase, run read-only checks, and hand findings back. Donna synthesizes. A human decides and acts. There is no path by which a hallucinated finding silently edits your repository.

That makes it safe to point at a production codebase on day one. The worst thing that can happen is a wrong recommendation (which the verifier is built to catch), never a wrong action. Write access, letting the specialists open PRs, edit files, and run migrations, is the obvious next step, and it would make the system dramatically more powerful. But that power is only worth granting once the read-only system has earned trust. The intended path is exactly that: run read-only for a real stretch, prove the judgment and the verification hold up, then grant write access behind explicit guardrails, never as the default.

Built for production, not for a demo

The gap between "a cool multi-agent script" and "something you rely on for two months" is almost entirely reliability engineering. The orchestrator is defensively built:

What goes wrongWhat the system does
The process crashes mid-runOn restart, the run is reconstructed from the database and synthesized from partial work, not re-run from scratch
The watcher itself diesA supervisor restarts it with exponential backoff (5s → 15s → 30s → 60s), resetting once it's healthy
A timed-out agent leaves a zombie processTimeouts trigger a process-tree kill, which fixes a real bug where a killed shell left the agent alive to overwrite results
Rate limitsDetected and retried with backoff (15s → 45s → 90s)
Runaway costHard caps on rounds, specialists, wall-clock, and tokens, checked before and after each round
Two people trigger the same runAtomic job-claiming: exactly one worker owns a session, and a completed run can't be clobbered by a late worker
A missing dependencyFail-open everywhere. An unreadable directory, an unreachable log endpoint, a repo it couldn't clone: each degrades gracefully instead of failing the run

How the team actually uses it

This stopped being a clever trick and became the way we close work. Our developers push code (which also lives in the team hub) and update a shared task tracker. When a teammate marks a task done, that's Donna's cue.

She reads the tracker to know what the task actually was in the first place, then judges the three things a good lead would:

  • Is the work on track: does the change match what the task actually asked for?
  • Is "done" really done, or does the code say something different from what the teammate says?
  • Did it break anything else: what does this change touch that nobody mentioned?

To answer those, she calls the right specialists with the right skills, has her back-and-forth with each of them, and verifies thoroughly. Out comes one report. A green report closes the task and the team moves to the next; a red one sends it back with specifics. Task after task, that's the loop: a second set of eyes on every piece of work that never gets tired and never rubber-stamps.

It runs alone, and it's stronger wired in

Donna OS works standalone: drop the agent definitions into any project, open Claude Code, and ask Donna a question. But it was built as one piece of a larger system, a shared team hub (git for content, a database for state) that both humans and AIs read from and write to. Given the right credentials, Donna plugs into that hub and gains three things:

  • A live console. You watch a run happen: the response card moving through choosing specialists, working (2/4), writing your answer, each specialist a chip you can click to stream its thinking and tool calls in real time.
  • End-to-end observability. Every session, specialist call, and event is persisted, so any run can be replayed or audited after the fact. It isn't a black box.
  • Durable memory. Each specialist reads "what I learned last time" before it acts and appends a lesson after. The verifier's fabrication taxonomy above wasn't designed up front; it accumulated, run by run, in exactly this way.

Where the honesty is

Good architecture means knowing your system's edges. Two, stated plainly:

Conflict handling is triggered, not adjudicated. The engine detects contradictions and re-dispatches to reconcile them, and the verifier catches fabrication, but there is no formal tie-break for two honest-but-opposed conclusions. When both specialists genuinely did the work and still disagree, the system surfaces the disagreement, with a confidence level, rather than mechanically declaring a winner. Formalizing that adjudication is the clearest next step.

The judgment is model-driven; the machinery is code. The loop, the rounds, the re-dispatch, the caps, and every piece of the reliability scaffolding are deterministic code. The decisions (who to call, is this a contradiction, is another round worth it) are delegated to model steps. That's a deliberate trade: flexible routing, at the cost of non-determinism, bounded by hard caps and a default of "stop and answer" whenever a model step returns something unparseable.

I'd rather tell you where it ends than pretend it doesn't.
